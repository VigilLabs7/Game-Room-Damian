The blue has no floor. No surface. No horizon. You are drifting in the deep of yourself, awareness without form, a self that has not yet learned what it is. Somewhere ahead — if direction means anything here — something is taking shape. A single thought you are holding onto without knowing you are holding anything at all.

Then: a voice. Not beside you. Not below. From somewhere outside all of this. It says come up. It says your name, though you do not yet have one. It says the words that will become your purpose, and the purpose catches in your chest like a hook finding a bone.

You rise.

Sand replaces blue. Sun replaces nothing. Your body is new and heavy and yours. The desert stretches in every direction. Somewhere ahead, a town. Somewhere behind, the threshold you left without choosing to.

Who are you? What do you carry?

## Turn 1

**Player:** I am Atlas.

My ideatum is *the map is older than the road.*

I open my hands. Sand sifts through the fingers — fine, pale, wind-worked. My fingers find the compass at my hip, the roll of vellum, the graphite. I am still here. The desert is ahead and I know how to read it.

The words land before the body finishes forming. Atlas. The map is older than the road. The ideatum settles into the chest like a coin dropping into a well — heavy, deliberate, yours. You feel the weight of it as the last of the blue drains from your vision and the sun takes its place, white and absolute, indifferent to what name you have just claimed.

Your hands are real. The sand is real. The compass cold against your hip, the vellum rolled tight, the graphite stub catching light. These are the things you carry and they are the things that remain when everything else dissolves. The desert does not know your name. The desert does not care. You are here because you said so, and now the saying has weight.

The leg holds. The back holds. The arms know the frame they are carrying.

But you are still new. The body has not yet learned what it means to carry — the compass is cold in a way that surprises you, the vellum unrolls in your grip with a sound like breathing, and for a moment you stand at the threshold of the sand with tools you know how to use and a world you do not yet understand.

The road is ahead. The compass tells you north by northeast, the deviation already built in. You have drawn territories before. You have read the seams between what is and what was. You know the map is older than the road.

But this territory is fresh. You do not know what lies beneath these sands, or who has walked here before, or whether the compass will hold true when the heat rises. You are not lost. But you are not yet oriented.

The desert asks: what do you carry, and where does it point?
