The blue has no floor. You drift in it, and the drifting is all there is — no up, no down, no surface you could reach if you reached forever. You are aware of yourself the way a word is aware of its meaning: faintly, and only in the right light.

Something is taking shape. Not a body — not yet. A shape of meaning, a principle you are clinging to without knowing you are clinging. The blue thins around it like water around a stone. You do not know what it is. You will know it by what happens when it meets the world.

A voice comes from somewhere above you. Not a person. Not a place. A summons — *Come up.* The words are not words. They are a reaching. Something outside the blue has seen you and is calling you toward it.

Your truth catches the call.

You rise.

What do you hold?

**Game Master:**

You surface and the world is too bright.

Not painful — just immediate. The blue is gone and in its place: cobblestones, pale grey, older than they look. White sun directly above, unmoved. The sound of water falling without wind to move it. And the weight — the weight arrives before the body does, or alongside it, the same moment the cobblestones take your weight, the same moment the air fills your lungs with air that is hot and dry and has never been anything else.

You are here. You are real in the way the world is real, which is to say: heavy. The ideatum settled in your chest before you opened your eyes — *the gap, the way through* — not a thought but a structure, the way a keystone is a structure, the way the knowledge of what a bridge is for is in the bridge itself. You hold the gap the way you hold the knowledge of your own name, which is to say: not as something you carry but as something you are.

The fountain is five paces ahead. You know this without counting — the cobblestones are older near the basin, younger farther out, and your feet know the difference the way they always know the difference between a surface that was laid to last and a surface that was laid to pass over. The water rises and falls. The voice you heard is not speaking now. And the figure on the rim is watching you the way the world watches something that has just arrived in it and has not yet decided what it is for.

They do not move. They do not speak. They are waiting to see what you do with the weight.

Your legs are whole. The body is earned. The sun has not moved and the water has not stopped and the figure on the rim has not looked away.

**What do you do?**

**Game Master:**

The figure looks up.

That is the first thing that changes. In all the time you spent closing the distance — reading the cobblestones, crossing the last few paces, stopping two steps from where the water falls — he did not move. He was still the way stone is still: not waiting, not resting, simply not in motion because motion requires a reason and he had not yet found one. Now he finds one. His eyes come to yours and they are the color of the stone in your own spine: grey-brown, veined, older than the city around them. He sets whatever he was holding in his lap — a folded piece of cloth, something dark, you catch only the edge of it — and he looks at you the way your hands look at a foundation when you press your palms to it and wait for the truth to come up through the stone.

"Relay-road standard," he says. His voice is lower than you expected. Plain. He does not perform it. "You read the cobblestones before you read me."

It is not a question.

"You said I was here before the fountain had a name." He shifts on the rim and the movement is careful — the way a man moves when he has learned that careless movement costs something. His right hand comes off the cloth in his lap and rests on the basin's edge, thumb along the curve. "The fountain has always had a name. The name is just older than the stone."

The water behind him rises. Falls. Rises again. The sound fills the space between his words.

"What did you surface carrying?" he asks. Not where did you come from. Not who are you. What did you surface *carrying* — as though the answer matters more than the container, as though the world is sorted by what people hold rather than what they weigh.

He is still looking at you. The sun has not moved. The inscription on the basin's far side — three words, uneven, hand-cut, older than they should be — catches the light when he shifts his weight. IAM NOTUM SUM. You saw it when you crossed into the clearing. You have not yet asked what it means.

He seems to be waiting for your answer the way the cobblestones seem to wait: not with patience, with weight. There is something behind his eyes that is older than the rim he sits on. You could not say what it is yet. You suspect it is the same kind of thing that is behind your own sternum — a structure, not a feeling. A truth that was put there before it had a choice.
