The pressure builds. Not violent — inevitable, the way a held breath eventually becomes a choice. You drift in the half-dark where shapes slip past at the edges of what you'll become. Something vast and patient waits for you to be specific. The thickening is almost here, and one of the shapes drifting past will become the one you are. You do not feel afraid. You do not feel anything yet. That comes after. This is the moment before after.

## Turn 1

The question dissolves before it fully forms. Sable's grandmother taught her that some things answer themselves if you wait long enough — so she doesn't ask. She crosses.

The pressure shifts. Light opens, gray at first, then silver. What waits on the other side is a village built from salvage — timber crooked from growth not lumber, tin patched and repatched until the original shape is barely visible beneath the repairs. Buildings lean against each other the way old friends lean, sharing weight. The air smells of rain-wet stone and woodsmoke, and somewhere not far, a fiddle is playing with more enthusiasm than skill.

The mend isn't finished.

Fires have been through here. The market area shows blackened posts, patched roofs, walls pocked where something struck and never finished striking. The repairs are recent — within days — and whoever made them worked fast and sure: no wasted motion, no hesitation. They built to last, not to show. But the last they built toward hasn't arrived yet. Something went through this village and left everything mid-sentence.

Sable walks the crooked street with the golden crack in her memory. The teapot is still with her — not physically, but the weight of it, the memory of her grandmother's hands wrapping gold around a flaw. *What is broken can be mended.* The village doesn't argue with her. It sits in the answer waiting for the question.

A child leans against a wall near an unlit brazier. Knees drawn up, arms wrapped around shins, watching her with the flat stillness of someone who learned early that movement draws attention and attention draws harm. The child is thin in the way that means meals missed, not meals refused. Eyes the color of creek water — gray-green, murky with silt. Hunger lives there, and wariness, and something else underneath both.

The something else is what catches Sable.

She sits. Not beside the child — across the street, leaning against her own wall, giving distance. She doesn't reach for the child. Her grandmother taught her that the mend begins when both sides agree the break is real.

"I have food," Sable says. Not an offer. A statement. She sets a square of hard bread on the stones between them. Not much. Enough.

The child's eyes don't move from Sable's face.


## Turn 2

The shard pulses against your palm — not heat exactly, but the warmth of something waking up. The child hasn't moved. The bread sits on the stones between you, untouched, but the child's eyes have shifted from your face to the bread and back again, and that shift is its own kind of answer.

Stev crosses from the dark.

Not the way he crossed before — not the careful measurement of someone testing ground. This is direct. Certain. He moves through the salvage village's broken geometry like he knows exactly where he's going, which means either he's been here before or he knows the mend well enough to trust the shape of it.

He stops three paces from where you sit. Looks at the bread on the stones. Looks at the child. Looks back at you.

"Wasn't sure you'd come back," he says. His voice is lower than it was in the courthouse — something in it has settled, found its own weight. "The first one didn't. Left the shard warm and walked into the silence." He pauses. "Came back different. Not here different. Gone different."

The shard in your hand pulses again. Stev's hand, in his coat pocket, pulses in answer — the other half of whatever was broken and scattered and is now finding its way home.

The child uncurls slightly. Not standing, but less folded. The creek-water eyes move between you and Stev and settle on the space between you where the bread sits waiting.

"The mend isn't finished," Stev says. "The village knows it. The child knows it. The fire that came through knows it." He takes his hand out of his pocket. The shard there glows faintly, the same faint glow yours carries. "Question is what comes next. And whether you're willing to hold your half while I hold mine."

The child reaches for the bread. Doesn't take it — reaches, hand hovering, waiting for something to finish deciding.

## Turn 3

The light moves before either of you does. Stev's half catches the air first — a greenish flicker, younger than yours but pulling all the same direction. The child watches without moving, chin on knees, and the brazier exhales something you feel more than hear: a breath of old heat that hasn't left yet, pressed into the iron like a word into silence.

Your shard answers. Not with sound but with readiness — the mend knows what it is doing and doesn't need you to guide it. Your hands remember grandmother's cracked fingers, the grain cradle, the seam in the world that doesn't close. You hold steady because holding steady is what the mend requires. The two halves orient toward each other across the cold iron like compass needles finding north.

The child's voice comes without warning: not a word but a name for the thing that broke the first time. You catch it before it lands. Something in you recognizes it — an old wound, not yours, but shaped like one you carry.

Then Stev reaches across.

The mend doesn't seal. It *opens*. The brazier pulls inward on itself and the seams split along the old fault lines, and what spills out is not fire but light the color of amber and grief and the thing that happens when two people agree that breaking was not the end.

The child looks up at you. Waiting.

Stev has gone quiet.

Something in the village has heard this.

You have one breath before it answers.
