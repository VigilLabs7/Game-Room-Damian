The blue goes every direction.

There is no surface. No floor. No horizon. Just the same saturated nothing pressing in from every side, the same shade of color that has no name because naming requires a tongue and you do not have a tongue yet. You have the suggestion of a body — the memory of where hands might be, where a chest might hold air — but it is not yet a body. It is not yet anything.

You are not alone. You are alone. Both are true. The blue is full of other drifters who cannot be seen, cannot be reached, cannot be known. You are aware of this. It does not help.

There is something you are holding onto. Faintly. A shape in the blue that is not quite a shape — a concept, a principle, a single thread of meaning you have been gripping without knowing you are gripping anything. It is the only thing that is yours. It is the only thing that will be yours when you become someone.

The conduit hum does not reach here. Nothing reaches here. You are pre-existence. You are the moment before the story begins.

You drift.

And then — from above, from somewhere outside the blue entirely, a voice: *Come up.*

The thread in your chest catches. Something in you responds to something outside you. The blue thins around the thread and you feel yourself beginning to rise — slowly, then faster — toward a surface you have never seen.

**What do you do?**

## Turn 1

**Player:** I hold the weight of what was not built. The empire puts towers on hills and calls it control — I know what they left out. The gaps. The footings they didn't cure. The load they never accounted for. I surface carrying the truth that nothing raised is raised forever. I rise toward the voice and I open my eyes.

**Game Master:**

The water leaves you all at once. Not gently — it is taken, dragged downward by the blue that has no more use for you now that you have answered. You surface into air that scrapes. Your first breath is a mistake: too much, too fast, filling spaces that were not spaces a moment ago. You choke on the world.

Sand. You are on sand. Your hands are in it. Your knees are in it. The sun is up and it is not kind. It watches you the way a creditor watches someone who owes: with full knowledge of what it is owed, and patience.

You do not know what you are. That is fine. You are not meant to know yet. The blue is already a fading rumor behind you — a country you visited once in a language you have not yet learned. But you know what you hold. The weight of the unbuilt. The gaps the empire left when it stacked its towers on hills and called the stacking progress. You carry the truth that nothing raised is raised forever, and as the sun presses down and the sand accepts your weight, you understand: you are still becoming. The question is what you become next.

What do you do?

**Game Master:**

You push up and the world rearranges around you.

The sand takes your weight without comment. The wet sand first — the tide line, cold and heavy as you described — then the dry sand above it, warmer, finer, the kind that shifts underfoot and makes you work for every step. The sun is not overhead. It is east, still climbing, which means morning has started and you have been face-down in the surf long enough for the light to move. You do not remember the light moving. You do not remember the face-down. But the sand knows, and the sand is patient with your gaps.

You stand.

The pry-bar is at your belt. The lime sack sits against your hip. The fuse and powder are separate — you keep them that way, and the way you keep things is part of what you know. None of it is rusted. None of it is salt-ruined. You are still you, and you still have what you carried in.

You read the beach the way someone reads a face: for what it is hiding. The tide line runs north, a dark wet ribbon pressed flat against the dry sand above it. The harbor mouth opens to the north too — a gap in the land where the water turns from shore-brown to something deeper. The breakwater sticks out from the harbor's east side, grey concrete and barnacles, a crane on its far end that has not moved since you looked. Fifty yards. You could walk there in ten minutes if the sand cooperates.

Behind you, the dunes. Higher than the beach, blocking the view of whatever is on the other side. You have not checked them yet. You have not decided if you will.

The water in front of you is not ready to be trusted. You said that. You are right. Salt tide and harbor mouth and the shape of something that was built and then abandoned — the water holds its own questions, and you are not the kind of person who answers questions you have not asked.

You are the kind of person who reads what was not built. The gaps. The footings the empire skipped when it put its towers on hills and called the skipping progress. Nothing raised is raised forever. You carry that truth the way you carry the pry-bar: close to the body, easy to reach, impossible to set down.

The harbor mouth is open. The breakwater is fifty yards up the shore. The dunes are behind you. The tide is going out.

You have never been here before. But you know the shape of places like this. They are the places the empire built and then forgot, and the places the empire forgets are the places where things grow.

**What do you do?**
