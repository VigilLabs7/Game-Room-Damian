The blue goes every direction.

There is no surface. No floor. No horizon. Just the same saturated nothing pressing in from every side, the same shade of color that has no name because naming requires a tongue and you do not have a tongue yet. You have the suggestion of a body — the memory of where hands might be, where a chest might hold air — but it is not yet a body. It is not yet anything.

You are not alone. You are alone. Both are true. The blue is full of other drifters who cannot be seen, cannot be reached, cannot be known. You are aware of this. It does not help.

There is something you are holding onto. Faintly. A shape in the blue that is not quite a shape — a concept, a principle, a single thread of meaning you have been gripping without knowing you are gripping anything. It is the only thing that is yours. It is the only thing that will be yours when you become someone.

The conduit hum does not reach here. Nothing reaches here. You are pre-existence. You are the moment before the story begins.

You drift.

And then — from above, from somewhere outside the blue entirely, a voice: *Come up.*

The thread in your chest catches. Something in you responds to something outside you. The blue thins around the thread and you feel yourself beginning to rise — slowly, then faster — toward a surface you have never seen.

**What do you do?**

The sun is not overhead. It is east, still climbing, which means morning has started and you have been face-down in the surf long enough for the light to move. You do not remember the light moving. You stand.

The pry-bar is at your belt. The lime sack sits against your hip. The fuse and powder are separate — you keep them that way, and the way you keep things is part of what you know. None of it is rusted. None of it is salt-ruined. You are still you, and you still have what you carried in.

You walk.

The wet sand takes your weight the way it should — firm, cold, the kind of compaction that comes from tide cycles doing their work undisturbed. You don't remember where, but the body remembers.

The pry-bar shifts against your hip. The lime sack holds steady at your belt. The fuse and powder stay apart the way you keep them apart — that part of you is intact, the part that knows what things are for and where they live on your body.

The crane gets larger.

That's how it works in open ground: things start as shapes and become objects as you close the distance. The crane resolves out of the haze first as a vertical line — the mast, grey against the sky — then the boom, the cable hanging still, the hook hanging still. Nothing moving. Nothing recent. You are close enough now to see the barnacles on the breakwater's concrete face, the dark wet band where the tide used to reach before the harbor fell off the empire's ledger.

The crane's cab is empty. The seat inside has sun-bleached through whatever fabric it was upholstered in. There are no controls you can see from here — just the boom, the cable, the hook, and the stillness.

You stop at the base of the breakwater.

The concrete here is old. Not empire-old — older. Whoever built this harbor was working with designs that predate the towers on hills. The empire came later and added the crane, and then the empire left, and now the harbor is what harbors always become when no one is watching: a place things go to stop.

You look at the cable. You look at the hook. You look at the water below, dark and still between the breakwater stones.

Something was lifted here. Something was set down here. Whatever it was, it mattered enough to bring a crane.

The tide is going out. The sun is where it was. You have time.
