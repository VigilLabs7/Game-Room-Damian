I find the first hold and the next and I descend into the dark, reading the chisel marks as I go. Someone cut these from below. Someone who was already underground when they built upward. The marks are old and they are patient and they go where the chamber does not. I follow.

**Game Master:**

The handholds change.

That is the first thing. You notice it when you are three notches down and your eyes have learned to read the dark — the marks you are following are the same marks, the same upward chisel direction, the same older grammar. But the spacing tightens. The cuts are closer together here, the way a staircase narrows as it descends. Whoever cut these was building for a body that was getting tired. Building faster. Building toward something.

The chamber's dressed stone gives way to rougher work — not sloppy, just different. The mason who dressed the upper chamber knew what he was doing; the mason who cut these handholds was in a hurry. You read the chisel marks as you go. A face emerges from the dark above you as you descend: not carved, not painted, just the way the stone catches the grey light from above. Eyes. A suggestion of shoulders. The marks are not random but they are not architectural either. Someone was here. Someone was here for a long time.

You drop the last hold.

The second chamber is smaller than the first — four paces across, the ceiling low enough that you cannot stand fully upright. The air here is different. Not cold the way the upper chamber was cold. Warm. The warmth of a space that holds what is left of something that once burned.

And the door.

This door is not set into the far wall the way the upper door is set. It is set into the floor.

A slab of the same dark material, flush with the stone around it, the frame carved with the same curving marks. A ring at the center — not a handle, a ring, iron or something that reads as iron in the dark, set into the slab as though the slab were a lid. The ring is warm. The marks around it are worn smooth, the way the candlestick circles on the altar above are worn smooth. Someone has lifted this door before. Many times. For a long time.

The floor around the slab is carved with the same circle-within-circle motif, two points at the center. But here the two points are connected by a line. A span. A gap that was meant to close and didn't.

The ring is warm under your hand. The marks are patient. Whatever is down there is still there.

Rook's voice comes from above, quiet, dry: "Still coming down?"

The ring does not move when you pull it. It turns. The slab does not lift. Something clicks somewhere beneath the stone — a mechanism, old, patient, waiting — and the sound echoes in the chamber like the sound of a lock that has not been turned in a very long time.

What do you do?
