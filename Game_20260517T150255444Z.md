His eyes cycle. Dim. Bright. Dim again — the blue trying to swallow what he wants to say, the way it swallowed the woman's words three seasons ago and swallowed whatever was in the music box. But here, now, in this store with the gold threading through the crack like a second seam, something is different. The gold is new. The mend is new. The shopkeeper's hands are flat on the counter and his shoulders are stone and his head has a patch and his eyes are trying.

He does not say anything. You do not say anything. The gold does what gold does when it finds the right crack — it fills, it holds, it spreads. The warmth in your palm matches the warmth in the wall. The teapot is warm at your hip. The music box is open and playing somewhere behind you, in a house down the road, in a room where a woman is learning that three seasons can end.

The shopkeeper's right hand moves. Slowly. It comes off the counter and moves toward the crack — toward where your hand is — and stops. Not touching you. Not quite. The hand hovers near the gold seam and then slowly withdraws.

You understand. Some mends are private. Some gold will not be watched while it works.

He looks at you. His eyes are bright — the brightest they have been. The blue is active and he is holding it back with everything he has and the gold is doing what the gold does and you are here and you are staying.

Some things mend quietly.
