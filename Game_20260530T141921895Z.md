**Game Master:**

The sand holds.

Not perfectly — the scrub here is sparse and stubborn, doing what it can with what the desert gives. But you find it. The ruts are shallow and widely spaced, the kind heavy carts leave when they are loaded and when they return empty. The traffic went both ways. Commerce. The column holds.

You walk and the column holds and the column does not sag in the middle — the way a bridge does when the middle support is weaker than the ends. Whatever this trade was, both sides had weight. Both sides had something to carry.

The heat-haze does the rest. The cobblestones appear first as a straightness in the wrongness of the desert floor — a line where the sand should be irregular and is not. Then the haze thins and the line resolves into individual stones, pale, fitted close, radiating outward from a center that is still too far to make out clearly.

The water reaches you before the cobblestones do. A sound that does not belong to the world you walked out of — the soft continuous chuckling of something that has always been here while you were still blue.

The fountain resolves. Stone basin, chest-high. Water silver and impossibly clear after the dryness of the desert. And on the rim: a figure.

Wiry. Stone in spine and joints. One knee skinned and bandaged. Eyes already taking inventory of you before you stop walking.

"You came through," he says. Not a question. His voice is quick and dry, the voice of someone who reads rooms the way other people read books. "Most people who come through the desert come through the fountain. The fountain is old. The town grew up around it. The clerk who ran it is gone, but the fountain is still here, and the water goes up and the water comes down and the water does not stop."

He looks at your hands. At the way you are holding your weight.

"You read the sand before you got here," he says. "That is not nothing. Most people walk until they hit cobblestones. You were looking for the column before you found it."

His ideatum sits behind his eyes: *the truth cannot be killed.* You can feel it the way you felt yours in the blue — a weight that belongs to you and that you belong to.

"The clerk was thorough," he says. "If you are the type who carries what the clerk left — the clerk left a ledger. Whatever the court buried, the clerk duplicated. It is somewhere in this town. The clerk was careful. Your call what to do with that."

He waits. The sun has not moved.

What do you do?
