The blue is all there is.

Not sky-blue, not sea-blue — something before those categories existed. Something older. You drift in it without drifting, because there is nothing to drift against and no direction to drift in. The blue holds you the way water holds a thought: completely, without pressure.

You are not drowning. You are not breathing. You are not anything yet — not a body, not a name, not a history. What you are is the faintest outline of a shape that hasn't decided what it wants to be. And underneath that outline, deeper than the blue itself, something is catching.

A single thought. Not words — the ghost of words. The shape of something you would do if you could do anything. A reason to become.

It is the only thing here with you.

The blue has no floor. No surface. No sound. You cannot feel your hands because there are no hands yet to feel. You cannot hear yourself think because thinking requires a skull and you don't have one. Only the concept remains — faint, formless, yours — holding on in the dark.

You cling to it without knowing you're clinging.

Something shifts in the blue. Not a movement — a frequency. As if somewhere far above you, or perhaps below you, or perhaps nowhere at all, a voice is trying to find you.

**What do you do?**

## Turn 1

**Player:** I am Sable.
My ideatum is: *What is broken can be mended.*
I carry the memory of a cracked teapot sealed with gold leaf — a grandmother's lesson that what holds together is not the whole but the seam. I arrived in the blue with hands built for mending and a limp on the right that doesn't quite articulate. The mend is already warm at my sternum. The thread pulls. I follow it upward.

You hold.

Not because you found a grip — because the mend is the grip. What is broken can be mended. The blue reads the shape of it and the blue does not dissolve it, because the mend is already holding itself together and the blue has no purchase on what is already whole. The thread at your sternum pulls upward and the blue thins where the thread pulls, and what is beneath the blue is not water. It is sand.

The blue does not break. It yields. You feel the yielding first as warmth below — dry, radiating upward through surfaces that are only now deciding what shape to take. Then as texture: grit against your palms, your knees, the point of your chin where gravity finally remembers you exist.

You are on your hands and knees. The mend at your chest pulses steady, not clinging anymore — warming, confirmed, yours. Your right leg aches with a pain that is already receding: the old injury, the grandmother's limp, carried through the blue and into the sand. You will walk with it. The mend reads the shape of the walking it permits.

Hands built for mending press flat against the warm sand. The mend glows against your sternum, and the blue above you is thinning, and somewhere ahead the thread is pulling toward grey stone at the limit of the heat-haze.

You follow it upward.

What do you do?

## Turn 2

**Player:** I push to my feet. The mend reads the stone ahead before I do — grey, patient, holding shapes that decided long ago what they were. I follow the thread toward it.

You follow the mend's reading the way a needle follows north: not because it must, but because it has already decided. The sand releases you the way sand releases everything it has held — grudgingly, with the particular friction of something that has no stake in whether you stay or go. Your right leg takes the weight and the leg is honest about what it costs. The mend reads the cost and the mend reads the paying and the mend does not intervene. This is what broken things do: they learn to walk and they walk and the walking is the mending.

The thread pulls.

The stone arrives before the sand finishes. Not the gradual transition of texture — a line, precise, the way a seam holds two different things together when neither of them asked to be joined. White sand to grey cobblestone. The mend crosses first and the mend reads the new surface the way it reads everything: origin ground, origin stone, the same stone that underlies what was built and what will be built on top of what was built. The cobblestones are warm. They have been in the sun long enough to forget they were ever cold.

You are standing at the edge of the town.

The mend reads the square before you see it fully — the particular warmth of a place that has been arrived at rather than passed through. The fountain is ahead, silver in the center, a figure on its rim. The four landmarks radiate outward from the square the way the cobblestones radiate outward from the fountain's center: store to the north, stable further, church with its lean steeple, the residential road south. The town is unfinished at its edges where it meets the desert. The town does not seem to mind.

Something about the mend reads the square and the reading is not alarm. The thread that has been pulling you since you stood up in the sand has reached the place it was pulling toward. The thread is warm now. The mend holds.

You walk into the town the way the mend read it: deliberate, measured, following what the ground has already decided is true.

The fountain resolves as you approach. Stone basin, silver water, IAM NOTUM SUM carved into the rim in letters that have been here long enough to stop pretending they are new. And on the rim — a figure. Broad shoulders, stone in the major joints, the particular stillness of someone who has been mapping this territory long enough to know which direction pulls.

He looks up as you cross the last cobblestones.

His eyes find the mend at your sternum before they find your face. Something in his face shifts — not surprise, something closer to recognition.

"The mend reads the seams," he says. His voice is quiet, plain, the voice of a man who does not waste words. "That's not nothing."

He does not ask your name. He does not ask what you are carrying. He reads what you are carrying the way the mend reads — fracture, seam, the place where one thing ends and another begins — and what he finds in you is: someone who knows how to follow a thread.

"Sit if you want," he says. "Or don't. The desert brought you here and the mend brought you here and the territory showed you enough to follow. That's not nothing."

The water is silver in the basin. The mend is warm against your sternum. The road to Lagota runs north. The residential strip runs south.

The thread has pulled you to the center of the square. Whatever comes next is the territory deciding what to draw on the map you are carrying.
