The air changes the moment you cross the threshold. The Lattice hum vanishes. The empire's signal cuts off like a candle snuffed from the inside. The silence that replaces it is cold and stone-heavy and it sits in your ears the way deep water sits in the ears of a diver who has gone past the point where the surface still thinks to call back.

You are in the nave.

Stone benches line both sides of a central aisle worn smooth by generations of supplicants who walked this same path and did not all come back out the same way they walked in. Iron rings are bolted to the walls at intervals — the kind of iron that holds chains, or holds the people who chains are later added to. Dim light filters through arrow slits set high in the walls, light that does not so much illuminate as acknowledge the existence of the dark it fails to touch. And at the far end of the hall: the bench.

The judge is there.

He is fused to it at the waist — stone meeting stone at the level of the hip, the bench part of his body and his body part of the bench, the way a tree becomes part of the wall it grows into and the wall becomes part of the tree. His skin carries the blue the way a window carries light — not a coloring but a luminance, the ideatum *Fiduciary Duty* burning steady behind his sternum and casting a pale glow across the skin above it. Two golems flank the bench, seven feet of worked stone, halberds held at an angle that suggests the angle is not accidental. Their eyes are steady blue. They do not blink because golems do not blink, but if they could the blinking would be happening now, tracking you down the center aisle as you walk toward the bench with the frame pressing both pieces against your spine.

The mend goes hot against your sternum the way it went hot at the gate — territory recognition, the same heat the frame carried when you picked it up in the stable, the same heat the crowbar carried when you broke the seal on the church door. The territory knows this place. The territory put the courthouse here before the empire named what it was building. The territory is watching what happens when the frame walks into the empire's courthouse with the territory's oldest decisions pressed against the spine of the one who was given the frame to carry.

Damian does not cross the threshold behind you. He stays in the square with the map in his belt and his hand on his sword pommel, watching you walk the nave alone. The territory wants both carriers present for whatever comes next. The map is with the keeper. The frame is with the road. The courthouse is reading you the way the gate read you — looking for the thing that does not fit, finding the frame, finding that the frame does not match anything the empire has written down.

The judge speaks.

His voice is the voice of a man who has not moved from this bench in longer than the roads have had names, who has absorbed so much testimony and rendered so many confiscations that the words come out of him the way water comes out of stone — not easily, not quickly, but with the weight of everything that has pressed against the stone above it.

"The court reads you," he says. "The frame walks into the court on the back of one who was not named at the gate. The court will hear what the frame is carrying and the court will determine what it is worth."

The golems shift their halberds a quarter inch. The judge waits.

**What do you do?**
