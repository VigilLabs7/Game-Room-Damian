The blue has no end. You drift in it and the blue is all there is — above, below, beside. No surface calls. No floor waits. You are alone with the sense of being, and the sense is formless, and the blue holds everything equally.

Something is taking shape in you. Not a body — not yet. A single truth, pressed against the inside of wherever your mind would be if you had one. You are holding it without knowing you are holding it. The blue thins around it, just slightly. Just enough.

A voice comes from somewhere far above. You do not know the words yet. You will.

What do you hold?

The blue is warm around me, and I am still deciding whether to rise toward the voice or let myself sink deeper into the shape of what I carry. I do not move yet. I let the truth settle.

**Game Master:**

The voice came from above. You heard it. The blue thinned at the place where the sound entered, and for a moment the shape of the call was visible — a thinning, a lightening, a direction.

You did not rise.

You let yourself drift a degree deeper instead. The warmth of the blue pressed closer. You felt the weight of what you carry settle into you like a coat you've worn so long you forgot it had weight. Chains, you said. You've worn them. You've sold the men who made them. You know the difference between a debt carried and a debt paid.

The debt settles deeper into you now. Not released — *held.* The blue accepts what you bring. You are what you owe, and what you owe is you, and that is a shape that does not dissolve.

The voice comes again. Closer this time. Higher. Almost in the room with you — if there were a room, if there were a you that had walls.

*Come up.*

You have been still long enough. The truth has settled. The blue is patient but the call is not patient — it is reaching, it is bright, it is the first thing in this place that has been *for* you rather than around you.

What do you do?

**Game Master:**

The blue parts for you. Not like water — like memory releasing what it no longer needs to hold. The voice fades behind you, already distant, already someone else's direction. Ahead, the thinning brightens. You move into it and the warmth of the debt deepens into something that has weight you can finally feel.

You break through.

Sand instead of blue. Bright instead of formless. You feel it before you understand it — the specific ache of a body that exists, face-down in warmth, the sun on your back like a hand pressing you into the earth. Something in your left leg fires sharp when you try to move it. Your left palm is scraped, bleeding a little into the sand.

You have weight. You have a wound. You have a self that has edges now.

The debt is inside you still. It is not chains anymore — it is hunger, thirst, the need to find a direction. But it is yours, and you are real, and the sand goes on in every direction with no path and no landmark and no voice.

Only the sun. Only the sand. Only the shape of what you owe, waiting to find out what it is worth.

What do you do?

You lift your head.

The desert is not featureless. You thought it was — you thought the sand went on in every direction without landmark or direction or the promise of anything that was not more of the same. But the sun is not overhead. The sun is east. It has moved since you arrived, which means time has passed, which means you have been face-down long enough for the light to shift, which means someone could have walked up to you and you would not have known.

No one did. You are alone with the sand and the sun and the debt.

You push yourself up. The left leg holds — it does not bend the way you want it to, but it holds. You are standing. The sand goes on in every direction, but one direction has something in it that is not sand: a dark line on the southern horizon, too straight to be a hill, too still to be a dust pillar. The line is far. You cannot see what it is from here. But it is there, and it is the only thing in the entire desert that is not sand and sun and the shape of your own breathing.

You do not know what the owing buys yet. You said you did not need the answer. You were right. The answer will come when the direction earns it.

You start walking.

The sand takes your footprints. The sun moves overhead. The dark line on the southern horizon grows — slowly, the way real things grow when they are not pretending. An hour passes. Two. The leg aches and you let it ache; the debt sits in your chest and you let it sit. The question you asked the sand — what does the owing buy — is still unanswered. The sand is not the kind of place that answers. The sand is the kind of place that makes you walk until you find something that does.

The line becomes a shape. The shape becomes a figure. The figure becomes a person sitting on something that holds water.

The fountain is ahead. Someone is at it.

**What do you do?**

## Turn 4

**Player:** I stop ten paces from the fountain. Close enough to speak. Far enough to run if the leg decides to stop working. The figure is still — not asleep, not dead, just still the way people get when they've been waiting for something they stopped expecting to arrive. "Anyone sitting on that stone is either guarding it or hiding from something," I say. "I'm not in a hurry to find out which until I know which one you are."

**Game Master:**

The figure looks up.

Not startled — rooks don't startle, in my experience of them, which is growing. He looks up the way someone does when they've been reading something dense and the room interrupts politely. Sharp eyes, wiry build, stone ridge along the spine. A skinned knee, the kind you get when you fall onto cobblestones and the cobblestones don't give. He's been here a while. The journal closed in his lap tells you that.

"You're asking which one I am," he says. His voice is quick and observational — the kind of voice that watches the edges of things. "Fair enough. Most people don't ask. Most people decide what I am and act accordingly."

He doesn't stand. The fountain rim is his, and he's keeping it.

"I'm not guarding it. The water doesn't need guarding — it's been rising since before either of us got here and it'll keep rising after we're gone. I'm not hiding either, exactly." He tilts his head. "I'm waiting."

"For what?"

"Couldn't tell you exactly. But I'll know it when it arrives. The water might be a clue. That inscription —" he gestures to the letters carved into the basin's outer face, letters you can see from here — "I haven't solved it yet. I've had time."

The leg. He noticed the leg when you stopped. The limp. The way weight lands wrong on sand.

"You're hurt," he says. Not a question. An observation. "The desert did that?"

The water rises behind him, silver and continuous, and the sound of it reaches you before anything else does — a soft, continuous chuckling that doesn't sound like it belongs in the same world as the silent sand.

"Most people come through the other direction," he adds. "From the town. You came from the desert. That's not typical." He closes the journal in his lap and sets it beside him on the rim. "I'm Rook. Just got here myself, more or less. I've got ten Hayeks and a sharp eye and I'm trying to figure out what any of this is. You look like someone who's got the same problem."

He waits.

What do you say?
