The blue is everything. You drift — no surface above, no floor below, no horizon in any direction. Only the same blue, extending forever in every way you turn. Something is taking shape in you — not yet a name, not yet a story, but a single truth you are holding without knowing why. The hum does not reach here. Nothing reaches here. You are alone, and the blue is patient.

What do you hold?

## Turn 1

**Player:** IAM NOTUM SUM
Mortar. The bridge-builder. The tower-dropper. Show me the footings and I'll show you where they cut corners — then I'll show you how to bring it down clean and teach the town to do it themselves next time.

**Game Master:**

You surface into light the way a diver surfaces — not gently, not by choice, but because your body remembers air even when your mind has forgotten it. The blue shatters. The desert takes you: white sand, red sun, a heat that presses down on your shoulders like a hand. No tumble. No leg injury. The body you wear was earned in a prior life, and it carries no wound save the one you brought with you.

The fountain clears the air before you reach it — the sound of water from somewhere ahead. You follow it. The cobblestones come first: a ring of them around a clearing, fresh and precise, out of place in the sand. Then the fountain itself, and the inscription carved deep into its basin wall — three Latin words, hand-cut and weathered: IAM NOTUM SUM.

He is sitting on the rim. Broad-shouldered, tall even seated. Stone ridge along the spine, stone in the major joints. A face that has the weight of someone who has carried a lot and put none of it down wrong. He has a book open in his hands but his eyes are on you — not suspicious, not welcoming. Testing. The way someone watches a new arrival who is not quite a stranger.

He sets the book down. Closes it. Looks at you across the inscription.

*"Mortar,"* he says, and his voice is plain. *"You come with the words and you come with a name. That's more than most. What do you build?"*

The town waits behind him — four landmarks visible from here, each of them new, none of them quite finished. The question is an invitation. Or a test. With him, it might be both.

What do you answer?

## Turn 2

**Player:** I fix what was built wrong, and I teach the fixing. Show me a wall, a tower, a bridge — I'll find the course they cut, the footing they skipped. I'll show you where the weak point is, how to shore it up or bring it down. That last part matters: I don't leave a place dependent on me. I leave them able to do for themselves.

**Game Master:**

The world settles around what Mortar said — not a grand declaration, but the steady answer of someone who knows what they're about. Damian studies him a moment longer, then stands from the fountain's edge, water still streaming from the basin behind him. He does not gesture toward the town so much as permit it to become visible: the four landmarks, none of them finished, all of them new in the way that means they were decided rather than grown.

He walks. Mortar follows or doesn't; Damian doesn't check. The cobblestones give way to packed dirt at the edge of what will be the general store — a frame, beams set and waiting, no walls yet. The general store in an empire town should have walls. This one doesn't, and the absence feels deliberate, the way a building site feels when someone is still deciding what the building is for.

*"They built fast,"* Damian says. Not a compliment. He stops at the base of the frame and runs his hand along a beam — testing it, reading it. *"Two days from nothing to this. The courthouse in Lagota took four. The courthouse here was supposed to take three. They cut the curing time on the mortar. You can see it if you know where to look — the joints are not set. If you wanted to bring that courthouse down, you find the joints first."*

He turns back to Mortar. The stone along his spine catches the light. His eyes are old in a way that has nothing to do with age.

*"You said you teach them to do it themselves. Good. But the empire will come back. The judge will come back. When they do, they will not care that the town learned to shim its own doorframes. What they care about is whether the courthouse stands. What they care about is whether the records are intact and the golem is fed. You can teach the town to fix what's broken — but do you know what breaks first?"*

The beam is solid under his hand. The rest of the frame will hold if the joints hold. The joints are the question.

What is Mortar's answer?
