Then Verath looks away, back toward the road, back to the watching that Verath does, and you are released.

You cross the square and take the fork south.

The residential strip opens differently than the commercial strip did — not wider but quieter, the cobblestones paler and less worn as though they were set down more recently or walked less. The houses are small and closed: doors shut, curtains drawn, the particular stillness of rooms where no one is currently expecting a visitor. One chimney smokes, faint and thin, the smoke rising without urgency into a sky that is beginning to go amber at the edges.

The dust on the road is undisturbed here — no fresh prints, no scuffs. Whatever direction the runner and shadow took through the interior, they did not come this way. Or they came this way and left no mark, which is itself a kind of information: a person who leaves no trace is a person who knows how to move.

You walk. The drop is warm against your hip. The tooth is in your coat, separate from the drop, connected by the warmth they share. The third house is ahead on your right — the one whose curtains moved, or seemed to, as you walked past. The smoke from its chimney is thinner than it was a moment ago. Or you imagine it is.

The road bends ahead, past the half-finished building with three walls and no door. The bend goes further into the residential strip — deeper into the row, toward whatever is at the end of it.

What do you do?
