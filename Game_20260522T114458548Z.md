The blue holds you.

Not sky-blue, not sea-blue — something before those categories had names. You drift in it, and the drifting is all there is. No surface above, no floor below, no sound except the silence that exists when nothing has yet decided what it's for.

You are aware of yourself the way a word is aware of its meaning — not yet fully, not yet finally, but with the faintest grip on what might make you real. Something is taking shape. A concept, a charge, a single thought you'll carry into whatever comes next. The blue waits for it to solidify.

The compass in your pocket — if you have one — points to nothing. The mend against your chest — if there is one — reads nothing yet. You are pre-language. Pre-history. The moment before the moment has a name.

But you will find a name. The blue always lets go eventually.

What are you holding onto?

## Turn 1

**Player:** I am Atlas.

My ideatum is *the map is older than the road.*

The compass came through the blue with me. The needle doesn't swing anymore — it sits east of north, holding steady. I carry vellum and graphite, a roll of cord knotted at five-pace intervals. The rest I left in the water. I go toward whatever answers.

The blue thins around Atlas like water finding a crack. The grip on the log — on the *map* — tightens and the tightens is what pulls him upward, the ideatum becoming a spine, the spine becoming a direction. The direction is north. The north is a place.

He falls.

Sand takes him the way sand takes everything that arrives here — not gently, not hard, just *suddenly* with the full weight of a body that was promised and then delivered. White sand, clean and directionless, horizon in every direction. The sun is not moving. The sun has never moved here. Atlas lies on his back and feels the sand hold his weight and feels the compass in his pocket and feels the mend against his sternum and all of it is confirming the same thing: the territory sent him, the territory sends everyone, the territory has been deciding where things go since before the roads had names.

His left ankle sends a flare of pain up through the shin. Sprained or worse — the fall was not soft and the sand did not cushion. He rolls onto his stomach and pushes up onto his hands and the ankle holds him the way damaged things hold you when you make them — imperfectly, with compensation, asking you to find a different weight distribution than the one you planned.

The frame is on his back. He did not lose it in the rise. The pen is in his belt. The vellum is in his pocket. The compass sits east of north, steady, the way compasses sit when they have already found what they are looking for.

Ahead — north — something glints against the white. Cobblestones at the edge of the sand, the first transition from the formless to the formed. The territory is already building the road.

Damian is not yet here. This is not that story. This is the beginning of this story — Atlas alone on the sand, compass steady, frame on his back, ankle singing, map in his head older than the territory that made the road.

He gets up. He favors the left leg. He starts walking toward the cobblestones.
