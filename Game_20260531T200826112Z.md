You are in the blue.

There is no surface. No floor. No horizon. The same blue in every direction, and you in it — not swimming, not sinking, simply being, without form or weight or name. Something brushes against the edge of what you are. A concept. A single truth you are holding without knowing you are holding anything at all.

The blue has no sound. No current. No others. You are alone here, and the aloneness is not cruel — it is simply the condition. You cannot reach sideways and find company. The blue does not answer when you call.

But something is taking shape. Faintly. A column in water, a ledger finding its rows. You grip it tighter and it clarifies. A principle. A reason to persist. This is what you will bring with you when the blue gives way.

Something calls from above. You may not hear it yet. When you do — when your truth catches the call — you will rise.

**What do you hold?**

## Turn 1

**Player:** I hold the names of the ones they put on walls — the ones a prefect wanted gone, not the ones who'd done anything. I learned to read who was really worth hunting. I picked my marks after that. That is what I bring with me. Someone always is the reason, and I carry that reason forward.

**Game Master:**

You said it. The words are not out loud — there is no air here to carry sound — but you said it inward, the way you have always said the things that matter, and the blue heard.

The blue does not answer in words. The blue answers in pressure — a slight give in the space around you, a sense that the formless expanse is attending. What you carry is not light. What you carry is weight: names on walls, the ones who were taken because someone decided they should be, the ones who did nothing but exist in the way that offended. You learned the difference. You learned to read it. The names are yours now — not carried in a book or a pocket, but carried in the way you move toward a room, the way you know what you are looking for before you enter it.

The blue takes this in. The blue does not give you the surface.

Something reaches down from above — warmth, a pull, a signal that knows your name even if you have not spoken it yet. It touches the weight in your chest and the weight answers. The names. The reason. The forward. The blue feels this exchange and the blue is not ready. You are close. You are holding what you came with and the surface is above you and the call is reaching for you and none of it is enough yet to open the floor.

Your edges are more defined than they were. Your hands are more than a shape in the blue. But the blue still has you, and the question it asks is not what you carry — you have answered that — it is whether what you carry is enough to carry you through.

You are barely holding on.

What do you do?

## Turn 2

**Player:** The blue asks if the weight will carry me. It already has. I step toward the light above — toward the call — and I bring the names with me.

**Game Master:**

The weight carries.

It carries because you said it would, and because the names are not a burden — they are a reason, and reasons have weight that bodies can lift. You step toward the light and the light receives you. The blue gives way not all at once but in the way a held breath gives way: gratefully, the body grateful for what comes next.

Sand meets your hands first. Then your knees. Then your chest. The sun is not yet a sun but it is already bright and already warm and you can feel it on your skin like a question that has finally found its answer. The sand is fine-grained and still, catching light that has no visible source, and you push yourself up from it and stand.

You are not cold. You are not hungry. You are not the person you were before the blue — you are the same person, carrying the same names, but the blue is gone and the sand is here and the weight in your chest has not lessened. It has found its place.

The names are still with you. You brought them through, as you said you would. The desert stretches in every direction — flat, white, vast — and at its edge where the light gathers, something is waiting. You do not know what. You will walk toward it and find out.

What are you thinking about?


## Turn 3

**Player:** I'm thinking about the road. Whoever put names on walls built this place — the desert didn't carve itself into roads and wells. There's a settlement at the edge of the light and I need to know if they're the ones who made the wanted lists or the ones the lists were made against. I'm walking north.

**Game Master:**

The sand learns your weight as you move.

You walk north and the desert does not try to stop you. The ground is firm where it needs to be and loose where it wants you to work for it, and the names in your chest keep their shape as you carry them. They do not weigh you down. They point you. Each step toward the settlement is a step toward the thing you need to know: who built the roads and wells, and what they did with the names of the ones who were hunted.

The sun does not move. You have noticed this. It has not moved since you stood up from the sand, and the light it casts has not changed since the first moment it arrived. The desert does not follow the rules you learned in whatever life you had before the blue. You do not pause to ask whether this matters. The settlement is closer than it was.

Two things happen in the same breath.

The sand ends. Not gradually — one stride you are on sand and the next you are on cobblestones, pale stone worn smooth, warm underfoot, the same material as the roads you were thinking about. The desert does not ease you in. The cobblestones simply start where the sand stops, and the change is clean and immediate, like a page turning.

And the sound changes. From the white silence of the sand to something you did not know you had been missing: the sound of water. Not rain. Not a pipe. Water moving the way water moves when it has been moving for a long time and is going somewhere.

The fountain is in front of you. Stone basin, chest-high, water rising from it in a column so clear it is almost invisible. The sound comes from there — a soft, continuous movement, like the water is speaking just below the threshold of words.

On the rim: a figure.

They are not solid the way you are solid. There is mist in the edges of them — a thin, grey veining that moves in the still air without seeming to belong to any breeze. The eyes are older than the face suggests. The hands are steady where they rest on the stone. They have been sitting here long enough to have become part of the fountain's scene.

They look up. They have been waiting.

"The columns must balance," they say. Not a greeting. A statement. Their voice is quiet and precise, each word weighed against something — a ledger, a column, a count. "Someone put names on walls. Someone has to carry them. That is the shape of it. That is the only shape it has ever been."

The water behind them rises and falls. The inscription on the fountain faces the desert: IAM NOTUM SUM. The cobblestones radiate outward in four directions. A town waits beyond them.

The figure on the rim does not introduce themselves. They are waiting to hear what you say.

What do you do?
