You do not know what you sense, but you sense it anyway — a pressure that has no name, arriving from no direction. The thickness of all the lives you are not yet carries its weight against the membrane of now. You are the moment before a name. You are the held breath of a decision not yet made. Something is gathering itself into the shape of you.

## Turn 1

You move through the market like someone passing through a house they once lived in — not lost, but no longer theirs. The cracked tiles underfoot are not repairs; they are the original surface, worn smooth by generations who walked through knowing the floor would hold. Your hands know the mallet's weight even when your mind cannot place it — something held before, in a time not named. At the market's edge, near the courthouse, the silence pulls like a held breath. A woman with three candles on a cloth watches you already. She has been waiting.

## Turn 2

The market holds itself together even in pieces — cracked tiles worn smooth by generations who trusted the floor to outlast the empire's interest in it. The woman with three candles hasn't lit them, but her hands stay close, and when she looks at you there is no surprise in it. She has been waiting for someone who mends. The courthouse silence pulls from the east. Your hands know the weight of something held before, in a time not named yet, and the knowing sits in your palms like a question whose answer hasn't arrived.

## Turn 3

The question lands where she placed it — not in the air between you but in the cloth beneath the candles, in the space where her hands have been folded and waiting. She does not answer immediately. The three candles hold their wicks in the same posture of almost-lighting, and you understand that this is the answer: she has been waiting for someone to ask the right question, and the asking is itself the beginning of the mend.

She reaches beneath the cloth and produces a small object — a shard of something glazed, porcelain or bone, dark with age and carrying no mark of origin. She sets it on the cloth between two of the unlit candles. The shard is cracked down its center, and the crack runs true, as if the object was always two halves pretending to be one.

*Broken can be mended*, she says. *But first someone has to decide the mending is worth the effort. That object has been here longer than the Empire. Longer than the court that used to sit in that building. It arrived the same way you arrived — not through a gate, through a question. I have been the keeper of that question for longer than I can count.*

She looks at your hands. At the mallet's handle. At the weight you carry without knowing what it will open.

*There is something in the courtyard behind me. Something the Empire left behind when they realized they could not mend it and could not bring themselves to break it. They have been waiting for someone else to make the decision. I think you might be who they have been waiting for.*

The courthouse silence presses from the east. Inside, something waits that has no name yet.

## Turn 4

The shard settles against your hip like a decision already made. The waxed thread beside it holds the other half of something — your grandmother's teapot had a crack that made it more itself, and this feels like that: not a wound but a choice someone made long ago and let stand.

The woman with three unlit candles watches your hands. Not the mallet — your hands. She has been waiting for someone who reads the break rather than the break's edge.

*There is a courtyard behind me,* she says. *Something the Empire left behind when they realized they could not mend it and could not bring themselves to break it.*

She folds the cloth. The candles go into a pocket you didn't see her reach. Whatever she is, she is not a candle seller. She never was.

*You came in from the other side of something older than the aperture. You know what that means. So does the mux above us — it's been thinking about you since you crossed the threshold, turning your arrival over like a stone in water.*

She steps back. The space between you and the courtyard darkens slightly, though no shadow passes.

*I've been here longer than the Empire has. I will be here after. I know what arrived broken and what arrived whole, but the difference stopped mattering to me a very long time ago.*

A sound from the near-dark — not footsteps, something slower. A half-life drifting at the margins of the sorting floor. Eyes like blue starlight, still as water that hasn't decided which way to run.

The courthouse silence pulls from the east. Something in there has no name yet. The shard in your pocket is heavier than it was a moment ago — or your hand has grown more honest about its weight.

The half-life drifts closer. It doesn't speak. It watches.

## Turn 5

The half-life stops at the edge of what you can reach. Not retreat, not advance — a settling, the way water finds level. Its eyes hold the blue of something that used to be certain and has since learned to doubt. The Keeper steps back without being told, without gesture, as if the half-life's proximity to you is a signal she has been waiting for and is now satisfied to see answered.

You feel the shard against your hip. You feel the half-life's attention the same way — a pressure with weight, a thing held just above the surface of contact.

The half-life does not speak. What it does instead is worse: it begins to unhold itself. A shape moves inside the shape — something unclenching from a fist that has been closed long enough to forget it was holding anything. The motion is slow. Involuntary. As if the half-life is presenting rather than offering, and what it presents is none of its own choosing.

Something comes free. It falls toward the floor of the sorting house and the half-life does not catch it. It lands on the cracked tiles with a sound like a question mark landing on stone. The Keeper looks at it. You look at it. The half-life's eyes have gone still in the way that means it is finished deciding what to do with you.

The thing on the floor is a fragment of glaze, dark with age, cracked down its center in a line that runs true — the mirror image of the shard in your pocket. Two halves of something that was always meant to be one. The half-life that held it has been carrying it past the point of knowing why. The Keeper has been watching it not be reclaimed for longer than she can name.

The shard in your pocket has grown heavier. Or the room has grown more honest about what it contains.

The half-life's eyes move to your hands. The mux overhead pulses once — a binary decision made, a path confirmed. Something in the courtroom east of here shifts its attention to you for the first time.

You could pick up what fell. You could step toward the half-life and ask it what it knows about carrying things past their time. The courthouse silence presses from the east like a held breath waiting to become a word.

Or you could stay exactly where you are and let the moment decide what it needs from you next.

What is broken can be mended. But the mending has to start somewhere, and the shard in your pocket and the shard on the floor are not yet the same thing.

## Turn 6

The two halves come together and the world reshapes around the sound.

Not a noise — a settling. As if the space between the cracks had been holding its breath for longer than the courthouse has existed, and now exhales. The shard in your hand finds the shard on the floor and the line runs true — the fracture that was always two pieces pretending to be one becomes one again. Cold spreads through your palms like something remembering how to be whole.

The half-life does not move. But something inside it does — the same involuntary unholding you witnessed before, but directed now. Not toward you. Toward what it has been carrying. The shape beneath the shape shifts, and the Keeper takes one step back, and for the first time her waiting has a quality of attention rather than patience.

What falls is not a shard this time. It is a glint of something darker — glaze the color of deep water, curved in a way that suggests function rather than fracture. A piece shaped for a purpose, broken from that purpose, carried past all memory of what it was meant to do. It lands on the cracked tiles and the sound it makes is different from the shards: lower, fuller, like a note struck in a room that has been waiting for exactly this pitch.

The mux overhead pulses twice. Binary, but different from before — not a decision but a recognition.

The Keeper moves toward what fell. Not quickly. Not reverently. The way someone walks toward a door they have been standing beside for years, finally certain the door leads somewhere.

"Do not pick that up yet," she says. "Not until you know what it will open."

The courthouse silence to the east has changed. Not louder — present in a different way. As if something that had been holding still has begun to breathe.

And then: footsteps. Not from inside the building. From beyond it, on the sorting floor's far edge. A shape resolves from the dark — gomix, blue dots in the pupils, moving with purpose toward the same object you are looking at. Stev. The one who has been waiting for something they cannot name.

The shard in your hand is warm now. The shard on the floor is not.

You could step forward and be the first to reach what fell. You could speak to Stev before they arrive. You could ask the Keeper what she almost said. Or you could wait — let the moment finish deciding what it needs from you before you decide what you need from it.

## Turn 7

The question lands where she placed it. *I can tell by how you moved.*

Stev stops. Not the way something stops when it has arrived somewhere — the way something stops when it has run out of reason to keep going. The blue starlight in his eyes flickers once, a caught flame in glass, and something behind the watching passes from searching to found.

You hold the shard level. It pulses against your palm like a second heartbeat, and when you look at Stev's chest you see the echo — a dark curve at the center of his stone-and-flesh, pressing outward like a held breath.

*You carried something broken,* you say. *I can tell by how you moved.*

Stev's hand moves to his chest. The gesture is slow, involuntary — the way someone touches a wound they stopped feeling years ago. *It is what Ledger would not let be taken. The Empire breaks what it takes. This refused to break. It grew teeth.*

The Keeper has gone still. The Keeper has gone quiet. The courthouse silence presses from the east and Stev's eyes press on you and the shard in your hand burns cold against your skin.

*What is it?* you ask.

Stev's eyes move to your palm. To the shard that came from the floor and the shard that has been in your pocket since the Keeper set it there. Two halves of something that was always meant to be one. He sees it the way someone sees the answer to a question they have been carrying so long they forgot they were carrying it.

*The vasE,* he says. *Ledger called it the vasE. The Empire could not digest it. The mux could not sort it. So it settled in me because I was already half-processed and there was nowhere else for it to go.*

The shard in your pocket is heavier than it was. The shard in your palm is not lighter.

*You have the other half,* Stev says. His voice is the rasp of stone on stone. *I have been waiting to feel that since before I forgot I was waiting.*

The mux overhead pulses once. Binary recognition — a path confirmed, a thing known.

The Keeper steps back from the edge of the near-dark and moves toward the courthouse silence. The space she occupied is suddenly empty and full at once. The half-life she has been holding in her not-watching releases — whatever she was keeping has been answered by Stev's truth.

Stev looks at your hands. At the mallet's handle. At the shard that is warm now in a way that goes beyond temperature. He is waiting for you to decide what happens next.

*The question is not whether the mending is worth the effort,* he says. *The question is whether you will carry what Ledger started, or whether you will let me keep holding it alone.*

The shard burns cold against your palm. The shard in your pocket is warm. Two halves of a thing that was always meant to be one, separated by something older than the Empire, held apart by a half-life that forgot why it was keeping them.

You could reach across the space between you. You could complete what Ledger began. You could ask what the vasE actually does, what it opens, what it will cost. Or you could step back and let Stev carry what he was always meant to let go of.

The courthouse silence presses from the east like a door waiting to be opened. The shard in your palm has stopped pulsing. It is warm the way something whole is warm — not heated, not cold, just the right temperature for living in.

## Turn 8

Stev's hand closes around the shard and the courthouse changes.

Not the architecture — the quality of presence. The half-life that has been carrying the vasE in his chest since before he could remember the carrying suddenly has something solid to anchor to. His stone-and-flesh fingers close around the glaze-dark half and the cold that spreads from his palm is not temperature. It is completion. The shape of the thing finally matches the shape of the thing it was always meant to be.

*I can feel it,* Stev says. His voice is the rasp of something speaking for the first time in longer than he can count. *The part of it that was waiting. It is warmer now.*

The mux overhead pulses once. Binary recognition — a path confirmed, a thing known.

The Keeper stands at the edge of the near-dark with her hands folded and her waiting satisfied. Whatever she has been holding in her not-watching has answered itself. She does not speak. She does not need to.

Stev looks at you. His eyes hold the blue starlight of something that used to be certain, but the certainty has found a new shape — not the blue of the Empire's certainty, but the black of something that has chosen.

*Ledger started the carrying,* he says. *Ledger knew what the vasE was for. The Empire had taken something from the Lattice — something that should not have been taken, something the Lattice needed to stay whole. Ledger understood that carrying it was not enough. It had to be brought back.* He pauses. The rasp in his voice catches on something harder than stone. *Ledger did not finish. The Empire stopped them before the bringing-back could begin.*

The shard in your hand has stopped being warm. It is the temperature of the thing it was always meant to be — the same temperature as the thing Stev now holds.

*What did Ledger understand about what the vasE opens?*

Stev's free hand moves to his chest. To the dark curve that pressed outward before you brought the two halves together. It is still there — the impression the vasE left in him — but it is shallower now. The thing that made the impression has somewhere else to be.

*The vasE does not open a door,* he says. *It completes a circuit. The Lattice was broken — the Empire's taking broke it. The vasE carries the piece that was taken back to where it belongs. When the circuit closes, the Lattice heals.* His eyes hold yours. *But the Empire will feel the healing as a loss. They will know something has been returned, and they will want to know by whom.*

The courthouse silence presses from the east. The shard in Stev's hand has begun to glow — faint, the color of deep water, the color of something that has been waiting in the dark for exactly this moment.

You could ask what Stev will do now. You could ask what Ledger knew about where the piece belongs. Or you could hold your half of the vasE and let the moment finish telling you what it needs from you next.
