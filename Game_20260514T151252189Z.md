## Turn 1

The weight presses from all sides — not the weight of flesh but of potential, each atom holding its breath. You are the space between one self and the next. The half-dark pulses with silhouettes that brush past like old memories with no face. Grandfather's voice surfaces through the static: the rivers don't follow the roads. The dust here is older than the maps that tried to name it. You came looking for the one that was here first. The thickening is almost here, and one of the shapes drifting past in the half-dark will become the one you are.

## Turn 2

The air tastes like water that remembers where it's been. You stand at a crossroads that has no sign — four paths diverging into scrubland, each one leading somewhere that looks like the last place and isn't. The ground hums under your boots, faint, like a pulse from below. Something brought you here. The rivers told you someone was waiting at the convergence point, and now the convergence point is around you and there is no one.

A sound reaches you from the eastern path — metal on stone, rhythmic, deliberate. Not combat. Something being worked. Shaped.

The light is doing something strange to the horizon. It doesn't set; it thins.

## Turn 3

The Map-Maker speaks.

Not to answer you — to finish a sentence the ground started. "You came from where roads begin," he says, and his stick traces a line that curves the long way around a shape you cannot see. "The rivers told you. And the rivers—" He stops. Looks at the dust where his line just was. Something in the shape of it doesn't satisfy him. He redraws it, and this time the line bends east toward Lagota and stops at a point that isn't a place yet.

The ground under your boots hums a note lower.

You feel it through the soles — not the lattice-hum of the conduits but something older, a vibration that predates the empire's infrastructure. The map-maker's lines aren't roads. They're channels. He's drawing where water used to run, and the water remembers Lagota even if Lagota has forgotten it.

"The courthouse pulls," he says. Not a question. He's been watching the same shift you have — the stick-figure at the courthouse end gone listening, Steve76 gone still in the way of something recording. "It pulls harder than the ground should let it. Something inside it has started speaking east." He taps his stick against the dust once, twice. The rhythm matches the metal-on-stone sound from the eastern path. "Or something east of it has started answering."

A third element arrives before you can name it: the dust at the crossroads edge shifts. Not wind — settlement. As if something beneath the surface exhaled and the ground compressed a quarter-inch everywhere the old river ran.

"You hear the rivers," the Map-Maker says. His voice has changed. Less patient now. More direct. "That's not common. Most who come through don't hear anything until the third or fourth listening, and some never hear at all." He looks at you the way a cartographer looks at a coastline that turned out to be longer than the charts said. "I was waiting for someone who would hear. Not for me. For the part of the map that doesn't fit in the box."

He extends his hand — not to shake, to point.

"East takes you to the sound. Down takes you to where the rivers still run under Lagota. The courthouse pulls either way."

The ground hums. The metal rhythm holds its beat. The Map-Maker waits for you to choose which map to follow.
