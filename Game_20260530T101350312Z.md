The blue is everything. Not dark, not bright — just blue, extending in every direction until direction itself loses meaning. You drift. You are aware that you are aware, which is the beginning of having a self, but the self has not yet settled into a shape you could point to.

Something is forming. Not you — not yet. But around you, in the blue, there is the suggestion of pattern. A cloud that only exists when someone looks at it. You are the one looking. As you watch, it takes a shape — yours. The seeing makes it so.

Above you — beyond the blue, outside it — something is looking back. Not a voice this time. A gaze. It finds you, and in finding you, you become findable. The cloud holds your shape and waits.

What do you do?

## Turn 1

**Player:** IAM NOTUM SUM. The cloud takes my shape — good. I've been a ghost before. I reach for it, and the reaching pulls me forward, out of the blue and into whatever this is. I open my eyes.

**Game Master:**

The words pass through you like a key turning in a lock that was always yours.

The blue does not part — it *recedes*, pulling back the way water pulls from a shore you were never standing on. You feel the threshold close behind you, the not-yet becoming was-never, and then there is stone under your palms and the sound of water in the air and the sun on your skin and the world is here and you are in it.

You are at a fountain. The basin is chest-high, circular, old. Water rises in the basin without sound — silver, impossibly clear after the dryness of everywhere else. The stone around the rim is worn smooth where hands have rested and lifted, rest and lift, the same grooves repeated until the stone remembers them. Three words are carved on the far side of the basin, letters hand-cut and uneven, weathered but not faded: IAM NOTUM SUM. They face the desert. They have always faced the desert.

Someone is sitting on the rim.

Not the way a person sits when they are resting — the way a person sits when they have decided that sitting is the thing they do here, and have been doing it long enough that the decision and the sitting are the same act. Stone plates across the shoulders, stone across the knuckles. A head wound still fresh at the hairline, skin torn around it, the particular rawness of someone who came through hard. Eyes that find you across the fountain's edge and hold there without urgency. A notebook in one hand. The pages inside are torn out — some of them.

The figure does not ask who you are. The figure does not ask what you are carrying. The figure looks at you and says, simply:

"The fountain called and you answered. That is the first thing. Everything else is the second thing."

The water rises. The inscription holds its place on the basin's far side. The figure waits with the patience of someone who has been here long enough to know that waiting is not the same as stillness.

What do you do?
