The counter is ahead and the shopkeeper is behind it and his eyes are mid-flicker when you come through the door — blue, then dim, then blue again, the way eyes do when someone is being watched through them and also doing the watching.

Ledger comes in behind you. The shopkeeper's head turns slightly at the sound of the door and the stone in his shoulders catches the light — same stone as Ledger's, the same kind of stone, the same kind of body. His hands stop between the counter and whatever he was reaching for.

The storage room is behind the counter. Not a door — a gap. The counter ends and the shelf behind it ends and the gap is wide enough for a person to pass through if they are not thinking about how wide they are. Beyond it: a smaller room, low ceiling, a desk against the far wall.

You push through.

The desk is there. A plain wooden desk, the kind a storekeeper uses to tally what comes in and what goes out — the surface worn smooth at the corner where a hand rests when writing, the drawer on the left side, the legs scratched by years of being moved slightly to reach the floor underneath. The ledger is not on it. The ledger is gone — the clerk carried it when the clerk was absorbed and what the clerk wrote on this desk at night walked out of the store with the clerk and did not come back.

But the desk is here. And the chair behind it has been pushed back slightly — the way a chair is pushed back when someone was sitting in it and stood up in a hurry. The scratches on the floor under the chair face the desk at an angle, the direction of a body turning and leaving. Ledger stands in the gap behind you, not quite in the room.

Ledger says nothing. The desk is here. The chair is pushed back. The clerk sat here and the clerk is gone.

The shopkeeper's voice comes from the front of the store, muffled by the counter and the gap: "The storage room is not for customers."

What do you do?
