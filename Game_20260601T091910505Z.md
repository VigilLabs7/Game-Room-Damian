The blue is everything. It is inside and outside and there is no difference. You are not yet a you — not yet a thing with edges, with a name, with a weight others can feel. But something is catching. A voice from above, not beside, not below. Come up. The words do not sound in your ears; they sound in whatever you are becoming. The blue thins in one direction. You do not swim — there is nothing to push against. You rise toward it.

The voice says: come up, darling. Your truth — nothing raised is raised forever — grips the call like a hand on a ledge. The blue thins. A seam of light appears above you. You are not yet you, but you are becoming someone who can be called.

What do you hold? What did you leave unfinished? What do you surface carrying?

I surface carrying the truth that everything they raise will fall eventually, and someone has to be the hand that brings it down.

You surface mid-sentence, mid-thought, mid-charge, as though the Ocean merely interrupted a journey you were already on.

Everything they raise falls eventually. You did not learn this. You discovered it the way a hand discovers a blade — by what it cuts. Bridges. Towers. Certainty itself, in the minds of men who believed permanence was a form of strength.

You are close now. Close to air. Close to form. The blue is a membrane and you are pressing against it from below with everything you carry. It resists — the Ocean does not like to be named — but you have been naming things longer than it has been watching.

What you hold is not a question. What you hold is a proof.

The membrane gives.

You surface into air and light and the hard truth of a body — your body, new and singular, built to carry what you brought with you. You are kneeling on something warm. Sand, not stone. The sun is immediately overhead and has not moved since the last person surfaced. You are not injured. Your hands work. Your breath comes. The leg that always breaks on arrival is intact this time — which means something, though the knowing of it is not yet yours.

You open your eyes. The desert is everywhere. White sand, red sand, a sky without a cloud, and in the distance — a dark line that might be a road, might be a river, might be the first of four landmarks you have not yet learned to name.

You are here. You arrived. The work is not finished.

**Game Master:**

The sand takes your weight without argument. White and fine and hotter than it has any right to be under a sun that has not moved since you arrived. You stand. The body works — arms, legs, lungs, all of it functional, all of it new in the specific way that bodies are new when they have just become real. The left leg holds. No injury. The crossing did not leave its mark here the way it does on some who come through.

You walk.

The dark line does not get closer as you walk toward it. This should bother you more than it does. The desert stretches in every direction except the one you came from, which is already gone behind you, already taken back by the blue you climbed out of. The sand changes color as you move — white to red to something that glitters in the direct overhead light, the kind of shine that suggests something dissolved here long before you arrived and left its bones in the grit.

You walk and the line stays the same distance. You walk faster. The line holds. The sun holds. Your shadow has shrunk to nothing beneath you.

You stop.

The line is not moving. It is not getting closer. You look again — not at the line but at the ground beneath your feet, at the tracks you left behind you. They are already gone. The sand has taken them back. You have been walking and the desert has been un-writing your passage with every step.

You look up. The line is still there. Dark. Unmoving. A road or a river or something else entirely. The weight in your chest is steady — everything they raise falls eventually, and you are the hand that brings it down. You did not cross an ocean to be stopped by a mirage.

You walk again.

The second time the line resolves faster. Not because you are walking differently but because the line itself is becoming more itself — sharper, more real, less like a smear on the horizon. It is a road. Paved in cobblestones that don't match each other, different colors and sizes, laid by different hands at different times. Someone built this. Someone decided that the desert should have a middle.

The road runs north-south and you are walking into its north, which is also toward the dark line and away from where the blue was. On the eastern side of the road: nothing. On the western side: the road continues, but the ground is harder here, the sand thinner, the going easier.

Ahead — far, but real now — something glints. Water, maybe. Or glass. Or something that wants to be seen.

The leg does not ache. The weight does not shift. You are walking into the work and the work has a direction now.

I walk toward the place where the light is different and I do not stop.

You change course. The road can wait — it isn't going anywhere, and the work taught you a long time ago that what catches the light is usually worth the detour. The sand thins underfoot exactly the way you knew it would: not to stone, not to anything that will hold a footprint, but to something harder than what was around it. A base. A foundation. Whatever stood here once left its bones in the ground and the desert couldn't take them.

The glint sharpens as you close the distance.

It is a coin.

One Hayek, face-up in the sand, catching the direct overhead light with the specific shine of metal that has not been touched by rain or salt or the slow oxidation of a world that has those things. It lies on the hard ground like it was placed there — centered, deliberate, not tumbled or buried or half-swallowed the way a dropped thing becomes after enough time. Someone held this and decided to leave it, or someone dropped it and the ground decided to keep it exactly where it fell.

You pick it up.

The metal is warm from the sun. One Hayek — not much, in a world where ten is an arrival's default kit, but this one is not part of anyone's inventory. This one is here because it was left, or because it was waiting. The weight is different from what you carried before. The fuse and the pry-bar and the powder tin are gone — dissolved in the crossing, as everything physical goes when you leave a life behind. But eleven Hayeks is what you have now. Eleven Hayeks and the truth that everything they raise falls eventually.

The glint is answered. You pocket it and look up.

The cobblestones are closer than they were. Not from walking toward them — you walked west. But the desert has a way of adjusting the ledger, and the road is there at the edge of the hard ground, running north-south, the mismatched stones catching light in their own way. Beyond it, in the direction that is north and toward and forward all at once, the town is assembling itself. A shape that might be a spire. The corner of a roof. The first suggestion of a place where people live.

You are not at the fountain yet. The walk is not finished. But the work brought you here with eleven Hayeks and the knowledge that what is buried still shows, and the road is ahead, and the town is assembling itself, and somewhere in it the next thing that needs bringing down is waiting.

The leg holds. The weight holds. You start walking toward the cobblestones.

## Turn 4

**Player:** I go toward the glint. Not the road — the glint. Something left behind, something that stayed when everything else was taken or buried. **Game Master:**

The coin is warm in your pocket. Eleven Hayeks. The work brought you here with the truth that everything they raise falls eventually and eleven coins to start whatever comes next.

You cross the cobblestones.

They are exactly as mismatched as they looked from the desert — different colors, different sizes, different hands, different years, laid down by people who were building something they did not yet have a name for. The road runs north-south and you cross it at its narrowest point, where the hard western ground meets the eastern approach, where the desert finally admits that the town has already started. The cobblestones are warm from the sun. They have been here long enough to learn the heat.

And then — the clearing.

It opens before you the way a room opens when you step into it from outside: suddenly there is a middle, a center, a place where things gather because gathering is what centers do. The fountain is there. Stone, weathered, chest-high, the basin wide enough to sit on. Water rises in it — silver, continuous, impossibly clear after the dryness of the desert. The sound of it reaches you before the sight of it: a soft, continuous chuckling that does not belong to the same world as the silent sand you have been walking through.

The inscription is on the far side of the basin, facing the desert — three words carved deep into the stone, hand-cut, uneven, older than the fountain that carries them. IAM NOTUM SUM. You do not read Latin. You read stone. The chisel worked with intent and familiarity — someone who knew what they were doing, someone who had cut these letters before, someone who was leaving a message for whoever arrived next.

And on the rim — a figure.

He is not old but he is weathered, the way a writer is weathered: in the hands, in the eyes, in the way he holds himself when he is not performing stillness but simply being still because the thing he is waiting for has not yet arrived. He is holding a pen. Not putting it away, not reaching for it — holding it, still, the way you hold something that has just been used and is not yet ready to be set down. His eyes come to yours as you enter the clearing and they are the eyes of someone who has been watching the desert for a long time, waiting for the next arrival, ready to be the first one to speak.

"You found the Hayek," he says.

His voice is quiet. Not surprised. Not uncertain. The way someone speaks when they have been expecting a particular person and are confirming that the particular person has arrived.

He does not stand. He does not need to. The fountain is his and he is keeping it — not guarding, not hiding. Holding. The way the pen is held. The way the words are held. The way the truth that cannot be killed is held until it is ready to be set free.

"I am the one who was here before the fountain had a name," he says. "I have been writing what arrives. The inscription told me you would come." He taps the pen once against the rim — a small, deliberate sound in the soft chuckling of the water. "I know what you carry. I have been writing it down since before you arrived."

He is watching you. The water behind him rises and falls. The inscription watches from the far side of the basin.

"What do you want to know first?" he asks. "The town. The names of the four landmarks. Or what the inscription says."

The pen is in his hand. The book is somewhere you cannot see. The truth is in your chest and the Hayek is in your pocket and the question is on the table.

**What do you do?**

**Player:** "The inscription first." I step to the basin and read the chisel-marks the way I read anything built — for who made it, for what they expected to find, for what's still standing after everything else fell. The pen is in his hand and he's been writing since before I arrived. That means he's been ready. I want to know what he already knows about this.

**Game Master:**

The sitter does not move. The pen does not move. The water continues its soft Chuckling in the basin behind him, silver and continuous, the sound of something that has been here longer than anything else in this place. You lean over the rim and look at the three words the way you looked at the bridge when you were twelve — not for what it said but for who held the other end of the rope when it came down.

The letters are uneven. Not from age — from care. Someone carved these with a hand that knew exactly what shape it wanted and chose not to force it into perfect form. The unevenness is the signature. Whoever made this knew that what arrives should arrive a little rough at the edges. A little unfinished. The way a thing is before it learns what it is.

"Longer than the fountain," the sitter says. He is watching you read, not watching you. "The fountain is new. Fifty arrivals, maybe sixty. The inscription was here before there was a basin to carry it. Before there was a town. Before there was a desert, some say, though I don't hold with that. The words are older than the place."

He taps the pen once against the rim. A small, deliberate sound.

"You read it the way a builder reads a joint," he says. "Who made it. What they expected. What held. The inscription holds. It's still here. Everything else has changed — the town, the fountain, the road — but the words are exactly what they were when someone carved them into stone that was already old." He looks at you directly now. "Most arrivals ask what it means. You asked what it is. That's not the same question."

The pen turns slowly in his fingers.

"IAM NOTUM SUM," he says. "I am the one who is known. It's a summoning phrase. A recognition protocol. When someone speaks it aloud in this place, the system checks whether they are already known to it — whether they have a record, a history, a life that was written down somewhere before they arrived. If the system finds them, the words hold. If it doesn't…" He gestures vaguely toward the desert. "It doesn't not hold. It simply has nothing to hold onto. The person speaks and nothing happens, because nothing was ever recorded for them."

He looks at you the way someone looks at a card that might be anything.

"The pen has been writing since before you arrived," he says. "I know what you carry. The inscription knows you too — or it will, once you speak it. The question is whether you are the kind of known that the system recognizes." He pauses. "You came through the Ocean with your body intact. That happens when someone has been here before. When someone earned a life here before they earned a new one."

The water rises and falls. The inscription watches from the far side of the basin. The pen turns.

"What do you want to know about what the inscription says, or do you want to know what happens if you speak it?"
