You are in the blue.

There is no surface. No floor. No horizon. The same blue in every direction, and you in it — not swimming, not sinking, simply being, without form or weight or name. Something brushes against the edge of what you are. A concept. A single truth you are holding without knowing you are holding anything at all.

The blue has no sound. No current. No others. You are alone here, and the aloneness is not cruel — it is simply the condition. You cannot reach sideways and find company. The blue does not answer when you call.

But something is taking shape. Faintly. A column in water, a ledger finding its rows. You grip it tighter and it clarifies. A principle. A reason to persist. This is what you will bring with you when the blue gives way.

Something calls from above. You may not hear it yet. When you do — when your truth catches the call — you will rise.

**What do you hold?**

## Turn 1

**Player:** Mortar. IAM NOTUM SUM.

**Game Master:**

The words land and the world assembles.

Not gradually — all at once, the way a structure settles: the weight arriving, the joints taking it, the whole thing finding its load path in a single moment. You are standing on cobblestones. The sun is a white point directly above, unmoved. A fountain at the center of a square, water lifting and falling without sound. And on the rim of the basin, mist and form — dark eyes, a face that is still deciding what it wants to be, shoulders that sharpen briefly and then thin again in the heat.

Mortar. The name you gave the blue and the blue gave back to you. The words you carry — *the span that was meant to close and didn't* — arrive with you, settled into your chest like keystones finding their arch.

The figure on the rim watches. The water rises behind them. The inscription on the basin's far side catches the light: IAM NOTUM SUM, carved deep, hand-cut, uneven — the work of someone who meant every letter.

The figure shifts on the rim. Does not stand. Turns toward you with the slow attention of someone who has been waiting.

"Mortar," the figure says. The voice is thin, used more for listening than speaking. "You came back different."

The square is quiet. The crossroads are bare dirt for now. Four landmarks wait at the edges — north, east, south, west — but the fountain is here and the figure is here and the question is not where you go but what you say to the one who was here before you.

The leg is whole. The body is earned.

**What do you say?**

## Turn 2

The way through." I touch the basin's rim where the inscription runs. "I've been reading foundations. The town — does it have powder?"

**Game Master:**

The question arrives before you finish asking it. Aethius is watching your hands as you move — not your face, your hands. The way you read a surface by the weight you put on it. The way you know a wall by how it takes a load.

"You read foundations," Aethius says. Not a question. A recognition. "That is what you carry." The mist in their edges has stilled. They are more present in this light than they were a moment ago. "What did you find in the blue?"

They gesture at the square around them — the crossroads, the four landmarks, the bare dirt between the fountain and the edges of town. The cobblestones radiate outward in four colors: red to the north, pale yellow to the east, white to the south, blue-grey to the west.

"Each quarter has its own foundation," Aethius says. "The roads between them were not built — they wore. The town grew around the fountain the way a ring grows around a tree. Each quarter is older than the last the closer you get to the center." They pause. "I have watched. I have never walked the quarters. I do not know what the foundations are made of."

The water behind them rises. Aethius looks at it the way a person looks at something they have seen many times and still do not understand.

"I can tell you what I have heard," Aethius says. "The north quarter — the red roads — was the first. The stones there are set deep. The builders who set them are not named in any book I have found. After the north, the east. Then the south. Then the west is the newest and the thinnest." They look back at you. "The oldest foundations hold the most weight. The newest ones hold the least. If you are looking for powder — for something that was never finished — that is where I would start."

The inscription on the rim catches the light again. IAM NOTUM SUM. You can feel the grooves under your fingertips. The work that went into cutting each letter.

"Or," Aethius says, and stops.

The square is quiet. The crossroads are still bare. The four directions wait.

"Or you could ask the wheel," Aethius says. "The wheel does not move here. But it turns in the people who live under it. That is what the wheel means — not motion but change. The wheel turns and the people feel it and they do not know why." They touch the rim near your hand. "You said you know what you are now. The gap. The way through. That is not a small thing to know. Someone who knows that about themselves might hear things the rest of us cannot."

## Turn 3

I tap the rim of the fountain twice — the way I check a wall for hollow spots. "That's pressure building somewhere with no release. A structure with stress and no joint to flex." I step toward the west quarter, the newest and thinnest. "I'll start where the foundation is weakest. Show me the wheel."

**Game Master:**

The west quarter is quieter than the square. The roads here are the newest — pale cobblestones, less worn, the gaps between them still sharp. The conduit hum is fainter here than it was at the fountain. The buildings are smaller, the doors closed, the windows either curtained or empty.

Aethius moves beside you without being asked. The mist of him has settled into something more deliberate — slower, watching your hands the way you read a wall.

You find the half-finished building set back from the road. Three walls, a roof, no fourth wall, no door. The packed earth floor is undisturbed except for one place near the back corner. Someone slept here recently. The impression is a bedroll's worth — head at the wall, feet toward the entrance. Two older impressions are pressed into the dust beside it, fainter, the edges already blurring. Whoever was here has been gone hours.

And the floor.

You crouch. Press your palm flat against the packed earth near the threshold. Then your knee, then your other hand, spreading your weight, reading the way you read a wall for hollow spots. The earth is dense and dry. Good compaction. But beneath the compaction —

Beneath it, a different resonance. A space. Not hollow — the resonance is wrong for a void. Something there that does not settle the same way as the earth around it.

Aethius has stopped at the entrance. Watching.

"The wheel," you said. The pressure building without release. The structure with stress and no joint to flex.

The earth here is holding something. Not weight. Something else.
