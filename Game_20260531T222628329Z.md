The crack between the stable back and the commercial strip wall narrows to shoulder-width, then less. Stone presses close on both sides, older mortar under your fingers, the kind that was mixed right the first time. The hum changes as you move — still split, but the underground frequency grows louder with every step, the other path fading behind you like a radio station leaving range. The blue glow is ahead. You can feel it before you see it: a drop in the pressure, a change in the quality of the dark ahead.

The woman moves without hesitation. She has done this before.

The gap opens into something that was not built — a passage carved by water or time or something else entirely, sloping downward at a grade that makes your wrong leg work harder than it wants to. The walls are raw stone, not fitted, the gap between them sealed by decades of silence. The blue glow comes from below. Not bright — a deep indigo pulse, the color of a bruise lit from within.

The woman stops at a corner where the passage turns. She presses her back to the stone and listens. You do the same. The hum is louder here — not just felt now but heard, a low frequency that vibrates in the teeth and the sternum. The blue glow pulses at the edge of your vision, steady as a heartbeat.

"It's not supposed to look like that," the woman says. Her voice is low. "When the relay tower is running, the glow is white. Clean. The blue means something broke the cycle."

She turns the corner. You follow.

The passage opens into a chamber.

The relay tower stands at its center — not the wooden relay structure you expected but something larger, older, built from the same raw stone as the chamber walls. Cables run from its base into the floor and walls, thick as your arm, pulsing with the indigo glow. The tower should be dark, the woman said. Three days. Something came through.

A sound reaches you from deeper in the chamber. Not the hum. Something else. A scrape. A drag. The sound of something heavy being moved through a space that was not designed for moving.

The woman freezes. Her hand goes to her pack. Not drawing — steadying.

The indigo glow pulses. The scrape comes again. Closer.
